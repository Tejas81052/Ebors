UNIT I

Fundamentals of Probability & Theorems

1.1Basic Concepts & Terminology

Probability is the branch of mathematics that measures how likely an event is to happen, on a scale from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). Before we can compute anything, we need a precise vocabulary. Each term below is the foundation for everything that follows — read them slowly.

Random Experiment

An action or process whose outcome cannot be predicted with certainty in advance, even though all possible results are known, and which can in principle be repeated under identical conditions.

Examples: tossing a coin, rolling a die, drawing a card from a shuffled deck, measuring tomorrow's rainfall.

The core building blocks

  • Trial — a single performance of the random experiment. Tossing a coin once is one trial.
  • Outcome (or sample point) — a single possible result of a trial. For one coin toss, "Heads" is an outcome.
  • Sample space \(S\) — the set of all possible outcomes of the experiment. The number of elements in it is written \(n(S)\).
  • Event \(E\) — any subset of the sample space \((E \subseteq S)\); i.e. a collection of outcomes we are interested in.
Simple vs. Compound events

A simple (elementary) event contains exactly one outcome, e.g. "getting a 5" when rolling a die: \(\{5\}\). A compound event contains more than one outcome, e.g. "getting an even number": \(\{2,4,6\}\).

Classifying events

Mutually Exclusive

Two events \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive (disjoint) if they cannot occur together — they share no outcomes:

\[A \cap B = \varnothing \quad\Rightarrow\quad P(A\cap B)=0\]

On one die, "even" \(\{2,4,6\}\) and "odd" \(\{1,3,5\}\) are mutually exclusive.

Equally Likely

Outcomes are equally likely if each has the same chance of occurring — no outcome is favoured over another.

On a fair die, all six faces \(1,2,\dots,6\) are equally likely (chance \(\tfrac16\) each). This assumption is what lets us use the classical formula below.

Exhaustive Events

A set of events is exhaustive if together they cover the entire sample space — at least one of them must occur:

\[A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \dots \cup A_n = S\]
Complementary Event

The complement \(A'\) (or \(\bar A\), \(A^c\)) is "\(A\) does not happen" — all outcomes not in \(A\):

\[A \cup A' = S,\quad A\cap A'=\varnothing\]

1.2Defining Probability

Classical (Mathematical) Definition

If a random experiment has \(n(S)\) outcomes that are equally likely, mutually exclusive and exhaustive, and an event \(E\) is favoured by \(n(E)\) of them, then

\[ P(E)=\frac{\text{Number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{Total number of outcomes}}=\frac{n(E)}{n(S)} \]

The axioms of probability

Modern probability is built on three rules (Kolmogorov's axioms). For any event \(A\) in sample space \(S\):

  1. Non-negativity: \(P(A)\ge 0\) — a probability is never negative.
  2. Certainty / Normalisation: \(P(S)=1\) — something in the sample space is certain to occur.
  3. Additivity: if \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive, \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)\).

Properties that follow

Range & certainty \[0 \le P(A) \le 1\]

\(P(\varnothing)=0\) (impossible event), \(P(S)=1\) (sure event).

Complement rule \[P(A')=1-P(A)\]

Often the fastest route: "at least one" problems are usually easier as \(1-P(\text{none})\).

Useful counting facts

A standard die has 6 faces. A pack of cards has 52 cards = 4 suits (♠ ♥ ♦ ♣) × 13 ranks; 26 red & 26 black; 12 face cards (J, Q, K); 4 aces. Two dice rolled together give \(6\times6=36\) equally likely ordered pairs.

Worked 1A single die — basic favourable counting

A fair die is rolled once. Find the probability of getting (a) a number greater than 4, (b) a number that is a multiple of 3.

Reveal full solution
  1. Write the sample space. Rolling one die: \(S=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\), so \(n(S)=6\). All outcomes are equally likely (fair die).
  2. (a) Identify favourable outcomes for "greater than 4". These are \(\{5,6\}\), so \(n(E_a)=2\).
  3. Apply the formula. \[P(E_a)=\frac{n(E_a)}{n(S)}=\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}\approx 0.333\]
  4. (b) Identify favourable outcomes for "multiple of 3". Multiples of 3 between 1 and 6 are \(\{3,6\}\), so \(n(E_b)=2\).
  5. Apply the formula. \[P(E_b)=\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}\approx 0.333\]
Answer \(P(\text{greater than }4)=\tfrac13\) and \(P(\text{multiple of }3)=\tfrac13\).
Worked 2Drawing a card — and using the complement

One card is drawn at random from a well-shuffled pack of 52 cards. Find the probability that the card is (a) a king, (b) a red card, (c) not a spade.

Reveal full solution
  1. Total outcomes. One card from 52 ⇒ \(n(S)=52\).
  2. (a) Kings. There are 4 kings, so \[P(\text{king})=\frac{4}{52}=\frac{1}{13}\approx 0.077\]
  3. (b) Red cards. Red = hearts (13) + diamonds (13) = 26, so \[P(\text{red})=\frac{26}{52}=\frac{1}{2}=0.5\]
  4. (c) "Not a spade" — use the complement. There are 13 spades, so \(P(\text{spade})=\tfrac{13}{52}=\tfrac14\). Therefore \[P(\text{not a spade})=1-P(\text{spade})=1-\frac{1}{4}=\frac{3}{4}=0.75\] Check by direct count: non-spades \(=52-13=39\), and \(39/52=3/4\). ✓
Answer (a) \(\tfrac{1}{13}\)  ·  (b) \(\tfrac12\)  ·  (c) \(\tfrac34\).

1.3Conditional Probability

Often we learn that one event has already happened, and we want the updated chance of a second event in light of that information. This is conditional probability, written \(P(A\mid B)\) and read "the probability of \(A\) given \(B\)."

Definition

For events \(A,B\) with \(P(B)>0\),

\[ P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \]

Intuition: once we know \(B\) occurred, \(B\) becomes our new (reduced) sample space. We then ask what fraction of that reduced space is also in \(A\).

Rearranging the definition immediately gives the multiplication rule (Section 1.6): \(P(A\cap B)=P(B)\,P(A\mid B)\). Symmetrically, \(P(B\mid A)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}\) when \(P(A)>0\).

Worked 3The two-children puzzle (the classic trap)

A family has two children. Given that at least one of them is a boy, what is the probability that both children are boys? (Assume boy/girl equally likely and births independent.)

Reveal full solution
  1. Write the sample space (order matters: elder, younger). \(S=\{BB,\,BG,\,GB,\,GG\}\), all equally likely, \(n(S)=4\).
  2. Define the events. Let \(B=\) "at least one boy" \(=\{BB,BG,GB\}\) and \(A=\) "both boys" \(=\{BB\}\).
  3. Find the needed probabilities. \[P(B)=\frac{3}{4},\qquad A\cap B=\{BB\}\;\Rightarrow\; P(A\cap B)=\frac{1}{4}\]
  4. Apply the conditional formula. \[P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}=\frac{1/4}{3/4}=\frac{1}{3}\]
  5. Why not ½? Knowing "at least one boy" eliminates only \(GG\), leaving the reduced space \(\{BB,BG,GB\}\) — three equally likely cases, of which one is \(BB\). Hence \(\tfrac13\), not \(\tfrac12\).
Answer \(P(\text{both boys}\mid \text{at least one boy})=\dfrac{1}{3}\).
Worked 4Conditional probability with cards

A card is drawn from a pack of 52. Given that the card drawn is a face card (J, Q, K), find the probability that it is a king.

Reveal full solution
  1. Set up events. \(F=\) "face card", \(K=\) "king".
  2. Count. Face cards: J, Q, K in 4 suits \(= 12\), so \(P(F)=\tfrac{12}{52}\). Every king is a face card, so \(K\cap F = \{4\text{ kings}\}\) and \(P(K\cap F)=\tfrac{4}{52}\).
  3. Apply the formula. \[P(K\mid F)=\frac{P(K\cap F)}{P(F)}=\frac{4/52}{12/52}=\frac{4}{12}=\frac{1}{3}\]
  4. Sanity check (reduced space). Given it's a face card, only 12 cards remain in play; 4 are kings ⇒ \(4/12=\tfrac13\). ✓
Answer \(P(\text{king}\mid \text{face card})=\dfrac{1}{3}\).

1.4Independent Events

Two events are independent when the occurrence of one has no effect on the probability of the other. Tossing a coin doesn't change what a die will show; they are independent.

Definition

Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if and only if

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A)\cdot P(B) \]

Equivalently (when probabilities are non-zero): \(P(A\mid B)=P(A)\) and \(P(B\mid A)=P(B)\) — knowing \(B\) tells you nothing new about \(A\).

Do NOT confuse "mutually exclusive" with "independent"

These are opposite ideas, not the same one.
• Mutually exclusive: \(P(A\cap B)=0\) — they can't both happen.
• Independent: \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\) — one doesn't influence the other.

If \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive and both have positive probability, they are necessarily dependent: knowing \(A\) occurred forces \(B\) to be impossible. So \(P(A)P(B)>0\) but \(P(A\cap B)=0\) — the two definitions clash, proving they can't be independent.

Worked 5Coin and die — independent events

A fair coin is tossed and a fair die is rolled. Find the probability of getting a head on the coin and a 6 on the die.

Reveal full solution
  1. Single-event probabilities. \(P(\text{Head})=\tfrac12\); \(P(\text{Six})=\tfrac16\).
  2. Independence check. The coin result cannot affect the die, so the events are independent — we may multiply.
  3. Apply the product rule for independent events. \[P(H\cap 6)=P(H)\cdot P(6)=\frac12\times\frac16=\frac{1}{12}\approx 0.083\]
  4. Verify by counting. The joint sample space has \(2\times6=12\) equally likely pairs; exactly one is \((H,6)\). So \(1/12\). ✓
Answer \(P(\text{Head and Six})=\dfrac{1}{12}\).

1.5Addition Theorem of Probability — Two Events

The addition theorem answers questions with the word "or" — the probability that at least one of two events occurs, i.e. \(P(A\cup B)\).

Statement (without proof)

For any two events \(A\) and \(B\) of a sample space \(S\):

\[ P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B) \]

We subtract \(P(A\cap B)\) because outcomes in the overlap were counted twice — once in \(P(A)\) and once in \(P(B)\).

Special case — mutually exclusive events

If \(A\) and \(B\) cannot occur together then \(A\cap B=\varnothing\), so \(P(A\cap B)=0\) and the rule simplifies to

\[ P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B) \]

Worked 6"King or heart" — events that overlap

A card is drawn from a pack of 52. Find the probability that it is a king or a heart.

Reveal full solution
  1. Define events. \(K=\) "king", \(H=\) "heart". We want \(P(K\cup H)\).
  2. Individual probabilities. \(P(K)=\tfrac{4}{52}\) (4 kings), \(P(H)=\tfrac{13}{52}\) (13 hearts).
  3. The overlap. The king of hearts is both a king and a heart, so \(K\cap H=\{\text{K}\heartsuit\}\) and \(P(K\cap H)=\tfrac{1}{52}\). The events are not mutually exclusive — we must subtract.
  4. Apply the addition theorem. \[P(K\cup H)=\frac{4}{52}+\frac{13}{52}-\frac{1}{52}=\frac{16}{52}=\frac{4}{13}\approx 0.308\]
Answer \(P(\text{king or heart})=\dfrac{4}{13}\).
Worked 7"Divisible by 3 or 5" — counting an overlap

A number is chosen at random from 1 to 20. Find the probability that it is divisible by 3 or by 5.

Reveal full solution
  1. Sample space. \(n(S)=20\).
  2. Event A: divisible by 3. \(\{3,6,9,12,15,18\}\Rightarrow n(A)=6\), so \(P(A)=\tfrac{6}{20}\).
  3. Event B: divisible by 5. \(\{5,10,15,20\}\Rightarrow n(B)=4\), so \(P(B)=\tfrac{4}{20}\).
  4. Overlap: divisible by 15. \(\{15\}\Rightarrow n(A\cap B)=1\), so \(P(A\cap B)=\tfrac{1}{20}\). (15 appears in both lists — count it once.)
  5. Apply the theorem. \[P(A\cup B)=\frac{6}{20}+\frac{4}{20}-\frac{1}{20}=\frac{9}{20}=0.45\]
Answer \(P(\text{divisible by 3 or 5})=\dfrac{9}{20}=0.45\).

1.6Multiplication Theorem of Probability — Two Events

The multiplication theorem answers questions with the word "and" — the probability that both events occur together, i.e. \(P(A\cap B)\).

Statement (without proof) — general case

For any two events \(A\) and \(B\):

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A)\cdot P(B\mid A)=P(B)\cdot P(A\mid B) \]

Read it as: probability the first happens, times the probability the second happens given the first already did.

Special case — independent events

If \(A\) and \(B\) are independent, then \(P(B\mid A)=P(B)\), so the rule becomes

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A)\cdot P(B) \]
"With" vs "without" replacement

When you draw items and replace them, draws are independent — use \(P(A)P(B)\). When you draw without replacement, the second draw depends on the first — you must use the conditional form \(P(A)\,P(B\mid A)\).

Worked 8Two kings drawn without replacement (dependent)

Two cards are drawn one after another without replacement from a pack of 52. Find the probability that both are kings.

Reveal full solution
  1. First card is a king. 4 kings out of 52: \[P(K_1)=\frac{4}{52}=\frac{1}{13}\]
  2. Second card is a king, given the first was. One king is gone and one card is gone, so 3 kings remain out of 51 cards: \[P(K_2\mid K_1)=\frac{3}{51}=\frac{1}{17}\]
  3. Apply the multiplication theorem (dependent draws). \[P(K_1\cap K_2)=P(K_1)\cdot P(K_2\mid K_1)=\frac{4}{52}\times\frac{3}{51}=\frac{12}{2652}=\frac{1}{221}\]
Answer \(P(\text{both kings})=\dfrac{1}{221}\approx 0.0045\).
Worked 9Coloured balls from a bag — without replacement

A bag contains 5 red and 3 blue balls. Two balls are drawn at random one by one without replacement. Find the probability that (a) both are red, (b) the first is red and the second is blue.

Reveal full solution
  1. Total balls. \(5+3=8\) balls.
  2. (a) First red. \(P(R_1)=\dfrac{5}{8}\).
  3. Second red given first red. Now 4 red of 7 remain: \(P(R_2\mid R_1)=\dfrac{4}{7}\).
  4. Multiply. \[P(\text{both red})=\frac{5}{8}\times\frac{4}{7}=\frac{20}{56}=\frac{5}{14}\approx 0.357\]
  5. (b) First red, then blue. \(P(R_1)=\dfrac{5}{8}\); after removing a red, 3 blue of 7 remain, so \(P(B_2\mid R_1)=\dfrac{3}{7}\). \[P(R_1\cap B_2)=\frac{5}{8}\times\frac{3}{7}=\frac{15}{56}\approx 0.268\]
Answer (a) \(\dfrac{5}{14}\)  ·  (b) \(\dfrac{15}{56}\).

1.7Addition & Multiplication Theorems for n Events

The two-event rules extend naturally to three or more events. The key new idea for the addition rule is the inclusion–exclusion principle: add the singles, subtract the pairs, add back the triple, and so on — alternating signs.

Addition theorem for 3 events

Statement (without proof) \[ \begin{aligned} P(A\cup B\cup C)=\;&P(A)+P(B)+P(C)\\ &-P(A\cap B)-P(B\cap C)-P(A\cap C)\\ &+P(A\cap B\cap C) \end{aligned} \]

General results for n events

Addition — mutually exclusive

If \(A_1,A_2,\dots,A_n\) are pairwise mutually exclusive:

\[ P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n}A_i\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(A_i) \]
Multiplication — independent

If \(A_1,A_2,\dots,A_n\) are mutually independent:

\[ P\!\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^{n}A_i\right)=\prod_{i=1}^{n}P(A_i) \]
Multiplication theorem for n events — general (chain rule) \[ P(A_1\cap A_2\cap\dots\cap A_n)=P(A_1)\,P(A_2\mid A_1)\,P(A_3\mid A_1\cap A_2)\cdots P(A_n\mid A_1\cap\dots\cap A_{n-1}) \]
Worked 10Newspaper readers — inclusion–exclusion for 3 events

In a town, 60% of people read newspaper A, 40% read B and 30% read C. Also 20% read A and B, 15% read B and C, 10% read A and C, and 5% read all three. What percentage read at least one of the three newspapers?

Reveal full solution
  1. Translate percentages to probabilities. \(P(A)=0.60,\;P(B)=0.40,\;P(C)=0.30\); \(P(A\cap B)=0.20,\;P(B\cap C)=0.15,\;P(A\cap C)=0.10\); \(P(A\cap B\cap C)=0.05\).
  2. "At least one" = the union. We need \(P(A\cup B\cup C)\), so we use the 3-event addition theorem.
  3. Add the singles. \(0.60+0.40+0.30=1.30\).
  4. Subtract the pairwise overlaps. \(1.30-(0.20+0.15+0.10)=1.30-0.45=0.85\).
  5. Add back the triple overlap. \(0.85+0.05=0.90\).
Answer \(P(A\cup B\cup C)=0.90\) — i.e. 90% read at least one newspaper.
Worked 11A problem given to three students — "at least one" via the complement

A problem is given to three students whose chances of solving it independently are \(\tfrac12,\ \tfrac13,\ \tfrac14\). Find the probability that the problem is solved (i.e. at least one student solves it).

Reveal full solution
  1. Set up "solves" probabilities. \(P(S_1)=\tfrac12,\ P(S_2)=\tfrac13,\ P(S_3)=\tfrac14\), and these are independent.
  2. Switch to the complement. "At least one solves it" is easiest as \(1-P(\text{none solve it})\). The students fail independently.
  3. Probability each fails. \[P(\bar S_1)=1-\tfrac12=\tfrac12,\quad P(\bar S_2)=1-\tfrac13=\tfrac23,\quad P(\bar S_3)=1-\tfrac14=\tfrac34\]
  4. Probability all three fail (multiply, independent). \[P(\text{none})=\frac12\times\frac23\times\frac34=\frac{6}{24}=\frac14\]
  5. Probability the problem is solved. \[P(\text{solved})=1-P(\text{none})=1-\frac14=\frac{3}{4}\]
Answer \(P(\text{problem solved})=\dfrac{3}{4}=0.75\).

1.8Theorem of Total Probability

Sometimes an event \(A\) can happen through several different "routes," and we know how likely \(A\) is along each route. The total probability theorem stitches these together. It is the essential set-up for Bayes' theorem that follows.

Partition of the sample space

Events \(B_1,B_2,\dots,B_n\) partition \(S\) if they are mutually exclusive (\(B_i\cap B_j=\varnothing\)) and exhaustive (\(B_1\cup\dots\cup B_n=S\)), with each \(P(B_i)>0\). Exactly one of them must occur.

Statement (without proof)

If \(B_1,\dots,B_n\) partition \(S\), then for any event \(A\):

\[ P(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(B_i)\,P(A\mid B_i)=P(B_1)P(A\mid B_1)+\dots+P(B_n)P(A\mid B_n) \]

In words: total chance of \(A\) = sum over every route of (chance of taking that route) × (chance of \(A\) along it).

Worked 12Two machines — probability an item is defective

In a factory, machine \(M_1\) makes 60% of the items and machine \(M_2\) makes 40%. Of \(M_1\)'s output 3% are defective; of \(M_2\)'s output 5% are defective. An item is picked at random from the total output. Find the probability that it is defective.

Reveal full solution
  1. Identify the partition (the "routes"). \(M_1\) and \(M_2\) are mutually exclusive and exhaustive: \(P(M_1)=0.60,\ P(M_2)=0.40\).
  2. Write the conditional ("defect along each route") probabilities. \(P(D\mid M_1)=0.03,\ P(D\mid M_2)=0.05\), where \(D=\) "defective."
  3. Apply total probability. \[P(D)=P(M_1)P(D\mid M_1)+P(M_2)P(D\mid M_2)\]
  4. Substitute and compute. \[P(D)=(0.60)(0.03)+(0.40)(0.05)=0.018+0.020=0.038\]
Answer \(P(\text{defective})=0.038\) — i.e. 3.8% of all output. We reuse this number in Worked 13 below.

1.9Bayes' Theorem

Total probability runs forwards: given the routes, find the chance of the outcome. Bayes' theorem runs backwards: we observe that the outcome \(A\) happened, and we ask which route was most likely responsible? It is the engine behind medical diagnosis, spam filters, and any time we update a belief after seeing evidence.

Statement (without proof)

Let \(B_1,B_2,\dots,B_n\) be a partition of the sample space \(S\) (mutually exclusive, exhaustive, each with non-zero probability), and let \(A\) be any event with \(P(A)>0\). Then for each \(k\):

\[ P(B_k\mid A)=\frac{P(B_k)\,P(A\mid B_k)}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(B_i)\,P(A\mid B_i)} \]

The denominator is exactly \(P(A)\) from the Total Probability theorem (§1.8) — so Bayes is really just \(P(B_k\mid A)=\dfrac{P(B_k\cap A)}{P(A)}\) with the top and bottom each expanded.

The four pieces — vocabulary

Every Bayes problem has the same four ingredients. Naming them makes any question easy to set up:

  • Prior \(P(B_k)\) — what we believed about route \(B_k\) before seeing the evidence.
  • Likelihood \(P(A\mid B_k)\) — how probable the evidence \(A\) is if \(B_k\) is the true route.
  • Evidence \(P(A)=\sum_i P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i)\) — the overall chance of seeing \(A\) at all (the normaliser).
  • Posterior \(P(B_k\mid A)\) — our updated belief about \(B_k\) after the evidence is in. This is the answer.

Slogan: posterior ∝ prior × likelihood, then divide by the evidence so all posteriors sum to 1.

A 4-step recipe that never fails

(1) List the routes \(B_i\) and their priors \(P(B_i)\).   (2) Write each likelihood \(P(A\mid B_i)\).   (3) Compute the evidence \(P(A)=\sum P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i)\) (this is just Total Probability).   (4) Divide the one route you care about by that total.

Worked 13Which machine made the defective item?

Continuing Worked 12: machines \(M_1,M_2\) make 60% and 40% of output, with defect rates 3% and 5%. An item is drawn at random and turns out to be defective. What is the probability it was made by machine \(M_2\)?

Reveal full solution
  1. Identify what is asked. We want \(P(M_2\mid D)\) — the machine given the defect. This is a "backwards" question, so use Bayes.
  2. Collect priors and likelihoods. Priors \(P(M_1)=0.60,\ P(M_2)=0.40\). Likelihoods \(P(D\mid M_1)=0.03,\ P(D\mid M_2)=0.05\).
  3. Evidence (already done in Worked 12). \[P(D)=(0.60)(0.03)+(0.40)(0.05)=0.018+0.020=0.038\]
  4. Apply Bayes — divide the \(M_2\) route by the total. \[P(M_2\mid D)=\frac{P(M_2)\,P(D\mid M_2)}{P(D)}=\frac{(0.40)(0.05)}{0.038}=\frac{0.020}{0.038}\]
  5. Compute. \(\dfrac{0.020}{0.038}=0.5263\ldots\)
Answer \(P(M_2\mid \text{defective})\approx \mathbf{0.526}\). Although \(M_2\) makes fewer items, its higher defect rate means it is responsible for the slim majority of defective ones.
Worked 14The famous medical-test paradox (base-rate fallacy)

A disease affects 1% of a population. A test has sensitivity 99% (it correctly flags 99% of sick people) and a false-positive rate of 5% (it wrongly flags 5% of healthy people). A randomly chosen person tests positive. What is the probability they actually have the disease?

Reveal full solution
  1. Define events. \(D=\) has disease, \(H=\) healthy, \(+\,=\) tests positive. We want \(P(D\mid +)\).
  2. Priors. \(P(D)=0.01,\ P(H)=0.99\).
  3. Likelihoods. \(P(+\mid D)=0.99\) (sensitivity); \(P(+\mid H)=0.05\) (false-positive rate).
  4. Evidence — total chance of a positive (Total Probability). \[P(+)=P(D)P(+\mid D)+P(H)P(+\mid H)=(0.01)(0.99)+(0.99)(0.05)\] \[P(+)=0.0099+0.0495=0.0594\]
  5. Apply Bayes. \[P(D\mid +)=\frac{P(D)P(+\mid D)}{P(+)}=\frac{0.0099}{0.0594}\]
  6. Compute. \(\dfrac{0.0099}{0.0594}=0.1667\ldots\)
Answer \(P(D\mid +)\approx \mathbf{0.167}\) — only about 16.7%. Despite a "99% accurate" test, a positive result means the disease is present only one time in six. Why? The disease is so rare that the few true positives (0.0099) are swamped by the many false positives (0.0495). This is the base-rate fallacy — set the widget above to these exact numbers to see it.
UNIT II

Random Variables & Probability Distributions

2.1Random Variables

In Unit I outcomes were things like "Heads" or "the 3 of clubs." Often we care about a number attached to the outcome — the count of heads, the sum on two dice, a waiting time. A random variable is exactly that: a rule that turns each outcome into a number.

Definition — Random variable

A random variable \(X\) is a real-valued function defined on the sample space \(S\); that is, \(X:S\to\mathbb{R}\) assigns a real number \(X(s)\) to every outcome \(s\in S\). We write \(X\) for the variable and lowercase \(x\) for a particular value it can take.

Example: toss a coin twice, \(S=\{HH,HT,TH,TT\}\). Let \(X=\) number of heads. Then \(X(HH)=2,\ X(HT)=X(TH)=1,\ X(TT)=0\), so \(X\) takes values in \(\{0,1,2\}\).

Discrete random variable

Takes a countable set of isolated values (finite, or a list like \(0,1,2,\dots\)). You can list its possible values one by one.

Number of heads, number of defective bulbs, dice sum, customers per hour.

Continuous random variable

Takes every value in an interval of the real line (uncountably many). Between any two values there are infinitely more.

Height, weight, time until a bus arrives, temperature.

The key distinction that drives everything

For a discrete variable we attach probability to individual points (using a probability mass function, §2.3) and we sum. For a continuous variable, any single point has probability zero, so we attach probability to intervals (using a probability density function, §2.4) and we integrate. Sum ↔ discrete, integral ↔ continuous — keep this pairing in mind for the whole unit.

2.2Functions of a Random Variable

If \(X\) is a random variable and \(g\) is an ordinary function, then \(Y=g(X)\) is again a random variable — it simply maps each outcome through \(X\) and then through \(g\). Common examples are \(Y=2X+3\), \(Y=X^2\), or \(Y=|X|\).

Definition — Function of a random variable

Given a random variable \(X\) and a real function \(g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\), the composition \(Y=g(X)\) is a new random variable with \(Y(s)=g\big(X(s)\big)\) for each outcome \(s\).

How to find the distribution of \(Y=g(X)\) — discrete case

Apply \(g\) to each value \(x\) of \(X\) to get the corresponding \(y\). The probability of a value of \(Y\) is the sum of probabilities of all \(x\)-values that map to it. If two different \(x\)'s give the same \(y\), add their probabilities together.

Worked 15Distribution of \(Y=X^2\) from a given pmf

A discrete random variable \(X\) takes values \(-1,0,1,2\) with probabilities \(0.2,\,0.3,\,0.1,\,0.4\). Let \(Y=X^2\). Find the probability distribution of \(Y\).

Reveal full solution
  1. Map each \(x\) through \(g(x)=x^2\). \(x=-1\Rightarrow y=1\); \(x=0\Rightarrow y=0\); \(x=1\Rightarrow y=1\); \(x=2\Rightarrow y=4\).
  2. List the distinct \(y\)-values. They are \(\{0,1,4\}\).
  3. Combine probabilities — note \(x=-1\) and \(x=1\) both give \(y=1\). So \(P(Y=1)=P(X=-1)+P(X=1)=0.2+0.1=0.3\).
  4. Read off the others directly. \(P(Y=0)=P(X=0)=0.3\); \(P(Y=4)=P(X=2)=0.4\).
  5. Check the total. \(0.3+0.3+0.4=1.0\) ✓
Answer \(Y\) takes values \(0,1,4\) with probabilities \(0.3,\,0.3,\,0.4\) respectively.

2.3Probability Mass Function (pmf)

For a discrete random variable, the probability mass function lists how much probability "mass" sits on each possible value. It is the complete fingerprint of a discrete distribution.

Definition — Probability mass function

The pmf of a discrete random variable \(X\) is the function

\[ p(x)=P(X=x) \]

giving the probability that \(X\) equals each particular value \(x\).

The two defining properties \[ \text{(1)}\quad p(x)\ge 0 \ \text{ for all } x \qquad\qquad \text{(2)}\quad \sum_{x} p(x)=1 \]

Property (1) says no probability is negative; property (2) says the masses over all possible values must add to exactly 1. Any table of non-negative numbers summing to 1 is a valid pmf — and finding an unknown constant is usually just enforcing property (2).

Worked 16Find the constant \(k\), then compute probabilities

A discrete random variable \(X\) has pmf \(p(x)=kx\) for \(x=1,2,3,4,5\) (and \(0\) otherwise). Find \(k\), then find \(P(X\le 2)\) and \(P(X\ge 4)\).

Reveal full solution
  1. Write out the masses. \(p(1)=k,\ p(2)=2k,\ p(3)=3k,\ p(4)=4k,\ p(5)=5k\).
  2. Enforce property (2): the masses sum to 1. \[\sum p(x)=k+2k+3k+4k+5k=15k=1\]
  3. Solve for \(k\). \[k=\frac{1}{15}\]
  4. Compute \(P(X\le 2)=p(1)+p(2)\). \[=\frac{1}{15}+\frac{2}{15}=\frac{3}{15}=\frac{1}{5}=0.2\]
  5. Compute \(P(X\ge 4)=p(4)+p(5)\). \[=\frac{4}{15}+\frac{5}{15}=\frac{9}{15}=\frac{3}{5}=0.6\]
Answer \(k=\dfrac{1}{15}\); \(P(X\le 2)=\dfrac{1}{5}=0.2\); \(P(X\ge 4)=\dfrac{3}{5}=0.6\).

2.4Probability Density Function (pdf)

For a continuous random variable, individual points carry zero probability, so we describe it with a density instead of masses. Probability becomes the area under the curve over an interval.

Definition — Probability density function

A function \(f(x)\) is the pdf of a continuous random variable \(X\) if the probability that \(X\) lies in any interval \([a,b]\) equals the area under \(f\) over that interval:

\[ P(a\le X\le b)=\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx \]
The two defining properties \[ \text{(1)}\quad f(x)\ge 0 \ \text{ for all } x \qquad\qquad \text{(2)}\quad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx=1 \]

\(f(x)\) can be greater than 1 (e.g. \(f(x)=2x\) on \([0,1]\) reaches 2). That is fine — only the area must stay in \([0,1]\). Also \(P(X=a)=\int_a^a f=0\) for any single point, so for continuous variables \(P(a\le X\le b)=P(a

The cumulative distribution function answers a single running question: "what is the probability that \(X\) is at most \(x\)?" It works for discrete and continuous variables, which makes it the most universal description of a distribution.

2.6Mathematical Expectation

The expectation (or expected value) of a random variable is its long-run average — the value you would converge to if you repeated the experiment endlessly and averaged the results. It is a single number that summarises the "centre" of a distribution.

Definition — Expectation \(E(X)\)

Discrete: each value is weighted by its probability mass and summed:

\[ E(X)=\sum_{x} x\,p(x) \]

Continuous: the sum becomes an integral against the density:

\[ E(X)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\,f(x)\,dx \]

\(E(X)\) is also called the mean and written \(\mu\). It need not be a value the variable can actually take (the mean number of heads in one toss is 0.5).

Expectation of a function \(g(X)\)

To average a function of \(X\), weight the function's values — you do not need the distribution of \(Y=g(X)\) first (the "law of the unconscious statistician"):

\[ E[g(X)]=\sum_{x} g(x)\,p(x)\qquad\text{or}\qquad E[g(X)]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x)\,f(x)\,dx \]
Properties of expectation (linearity) \[ E(c)=c \qquad E(aX)=a\,E(X) \qquad E(aX+b)=a\,E(X)+b \] \[ E(X+Y)=E(X)+E(Y)\ \text{ (always)} \]

Linearity is the workhorse of the whole subject: the expectation of a sum is the sum of expectations, whether or not the variables are independent, and constants pull straight out.

Worked 20Expected value of a game (fair or not?)

You roll a fair die. You win the rupee-amount equal to the number shown, but it costs 4 rupees to play. Let \(X\) be the number rolled. Find \(E(X)\), then the expected net gain \(E(X-4)\). Is the game fair?

Reveal full solution
  1. Write the pmf. \(X\in\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\), each with probability \(\tfrac16\).
  2. Apply \(E(X)=\sum x\,p(x)\). \[E(X)=\frac{1}{6}(1+2+3+4+5+6)=\frac{1}{6}\cdot 21=\frac{21}{6}=3.5\]
  3. Use linearity for the net gain \(E(X-4)\). \[E(X-4)=E(X)-4=3.5-4=-0.5\]
  4. Interpret. The expected net gain is \(-0.5\) rupees per play.
Answer \(E(X)=3.5\); expected net gain \(=-0.50\) rupees. The game is not fair — on average you lose half a rupee each time. (A fair entry fee would be exactly 3.50 rupees.)

2.7Mean & Variance — Discrete

The mean tells us where a distribution is centred; the variance tells us how spread out it is around that centre. For a discrete variable both are weighted sums against the pmf.

Mean and variance — discrete

Mean: \(\displaystyle \mu=E(X)=\sum_x x\,p(x)\).

Variance: the expected squared distance from the mean,

\[ \sigma^2=\operatorname{Var}(X)=E\big[(X-\mu)^2\big]=\sum_x (x-\mu)^2\,p(x) \]

Standard deviation: \(\sigma=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}\) — the spread in the same units as \(X\).

The shortcut formula (use this in practice) \[ \operatorname{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-\big[E(X)\big]^2 \qquad\text{where}\qquad E(X^2)=\sum_x x^2\,p(x) \]

This "mean of the square minus the square of the mean" form is almost always faster than the definition, because you avoid subtracting \(\mu\) inside every term. Compute \(E(X)\) and \(E(X^2)\) in two passes, then subtract.

2.8Mean & Variance — Continuous

For a continuous variable the recipe is identical to the discrete case with one swap: every sum becomes an integral, and the pmf \(p(x)\) becomes the density \(f(x)\). The shortcut variance formula survives unchanged.

Mean and variance — continuous \[ \mu=E(X)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\,f(x)\,dx \] \[ E(X^2)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^2 f(x)\,dx \qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-\big[E(X)\big]^2 \]

Integrate only over the region where \(f(x)\neq 0\) — the support of the density.
